# NRG Hero Slogan — Top 10

---
## 1. 8.8/10

**Not a tech guy.**
**Hired one anyway.**

**Why it works:** The self-deprecating voice is exactly how Noah's target customer thinks — this isn't aspirational, it's a mirror. "Hired one anyway" sweeps in as the cyan block hits it and reads like a punch line delivered at full billboard scale. It also positions NRG as the smart move rather than a scary tech purchase.

**Potential concern:** Slightly self-effacing framing could undercut premium positioning if NRG ever raises prices significantly — it implies the customer is doing them a favor.

**Best for:** Any small business owner who has resisted building a website for years; especially strong for trades (plumber, HVAC, electrician) and owner-operated service businesses.

---
## 2. 8.8/10

**You fix the AC.**
**We fix your website.**

**Why it works:** The parallel structure is instantly ownable — NRG could run this as a campaign with dozens of industry swaps (you cook the food, you cut the hair). The final line "We fix your website" lands hardest under the cyan sweep because it's a direct value promise with zero filler words. It speaks peer-to-peer, one trade to another.

**Potential concern:** The word "fix" implies the customer already has a broken site — it may not land as well for a business that has no site at all.

**Best for:** Houston trades and service businesses: HVAC, plumbers, electricians, contractors — the most literal reading of the slogan matches their work.

---
## 3. 8.8/10

**You cook the food.**
**We bring them in.**

**Why it works:** "We bring them in" is the most outcome-focused last line in the entire list — it promises customers, not a deliverable. At large display size, five monosyllables swept by cyan feels like a door swinging open. The food/restaurant context also broadens NRG's visual into one of Houston's most visible small business categories.

**Potential concern:** Less ownable than the AC variant because the food-service metaphor is common in marketing; a competing agency could run a similar line without it feeling stolen.

**Best for:** Houston restaurants, caterers, food trucks, and any hospitality business — though the underlying logic works for anyone.

---
## 4. 8.3/10

**Forty calls a week.**
**You're only getting five.**

**Why it works:** This is the most confrontational option — it names the loss in concrete, countable terms. The gap between 40 and 5 is visceral. The second line under the cyan sweep reads like an indictment that turns into an invitation. It will stop the scroll because the numbers feel specific enough to be real.

**Potential concern:** The numbers are invented, and a skeptical reader might question them. If NRG ever makes a factual claim elsewhere, this line could feel inconsistent.

**Best for:** Business owners who are already frustrated with their online presence and suspect they are losing work — the self-aware customer who clicks because the line named their problem exactly.

---
## 5. 8.3/10

**They looked you up.**
**They called someone else.**

**Why it works:** Two lines, two sentences, one gut punch. The past tense makes the loss feel like it already happened — and for most of Noah's customers, it has. "Someone else" is more damaging than any statistic. As the cyan sweeps across "They called someone else," the inversion (white-to-black text) makes those four words look like a verdict.

**Potential concern:** The tone is the most anxiety-inducing of the set — it could feel accusatory rather than empowering if the page design doesn't immediately offer the solution below the fold.

**Best for:** Any business owner who has ever Googled themselves and not liked what they found; particularly effective for established businesses with no site or an outdated one.

---
## 6. 8.3/10

**Search your business.**
**Find your site.**
**Book the job.**

**Why it works:** The only 3-line sequence with a 9/10 Design Fit — the rater recognized that each line is a discrete visual beat, which is exactly what the staggered sweep animation is built for. Three short imperative phrases read as a tutorial and a promise simultaneously. "Book the job" as the final sweeping line is the strongest possible payoff word in trades contexts.

**Potential concern:** Reads slightly instructional — it describes the user journey rather than making an emotional claim, which is less bold than the top two-line options.

**Best for:** Trades businesses where the explicit outcome is a booked appointment: HVAC, plumbers, electricians, salons, law offices — any business where the conversion event is a booking or call.

---
## 7. 8.0/10

**Not a template.**
**Your actual business.**

**Why it works:** This is the differentiation argument — it speaks directly to the fear that web design is a commodity. "Your actual business" as the final sweep line is a quiet promise that cuts against every DIY website builder ad the customer has ever seen. It positions NRG on quality without using the word "quality."

**Potential concern:** It's a features argument, not an outcomes argument — it tells the customer what NRG builds, not what changes for them. Less visceral than the loss-focused options.

**Best for:** Business owners who have already tried Wix, Squarespace, or GoDaddy and been disappointed — the second-time buyer who knows what generic looks like.

---
## 8. 8.0/10

**Forms filled.**
**Texts sent.**
**You wake up to leads.**

**Why it works:** The most concrete sequence in the list — it describes the automation loop in three operational steps and lands on the most desirable morning any small business owner can imagine. "You wake up to leads" under the cyan sweep is the closest thing to a billboard promise in the set. Three lines give the animation three distinct beats to work with.

**Potential concern:** "Forms filled. Texts sent." may set expectations for automation features that NRG's standard offering doesn't include — worth confirming the service scope before leading with this.

**Best for:** Service businesses that rely on lead capture: law offices, insurance agents, contractors bidding jobs — any business where the pipeline starts with an inquiry form.

---
## 9. 8.0/10

**The site you've been**
**putting off.**

**Why it works:** This is the most intimate line in the set. It doesn't describe a product or a result — it names a feeling of procrastination that every small business owner has had. The line break after "been" creates a natural pause before "putting off" arrives like a knowing exhale. Under the sweep, "putting off" reads as both an acknowledgment and a gentle challenge.

**Potential concern:** Lowest ownability of the top options — the line doesn't contain NRG's brand logic, so it could be said by any freelancer or agency without feeling off. Hard to build a campaign around it.

**Best for:** First-time website buyers who feel guilty or embarrassed about not having a site yet — a softer entry point than the loss-focused options.

---
## 10. 7.8/10

**Two weeks.**
**One less problem.**

**Why it works:** Maximum compression — four words total, zero waste. "One less problem" as the final sweep line is colloquial and confident without being flashy. It reframes the website as a problem solved rather than a product purchased, which is exactly how a busy plumber or salon owner thinks. The brevity also signals competence: NRG doesn't need to explain itself.

**Potential concern:** Undersells the outcome — "one less problem" is a relief frame, not an aspiration frame. It may attract customers who want to check a box rather than customers who want to grow.

**Best for:** Extremely busy owner-operators who have zero patience for sales language — the person who wants to be told the minimum they need to know and then move on.

---

## Noah's Decision Guide

Three questions to help Noah choose:

1. Which one would you say out loud to a client on a first call?
2. Which one looks most dramatic as the cyan sweeps across it?
3. Which one would you still like in 2 years?

The answer to all three pointing at the same slogan is the one to use.
